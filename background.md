# **Literature Review**

Recent research has increasingly examined how individuals use large language model (LLM) chatbots for mental health support, particularly in contexts where traditional care is inaccessible, stigmatized, or insufficient. Across qualitative and mixed-method studies, evidence suggests that users engage with AI systems in ways that closely mirror core therapeutic processes, including emotional expression, meaning-making, and interpersonal problem-solving (Siddals et al., 2024; Song et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). The categories proposed in the present framework are largely consistent with patterns identified across this emerging literature.

## **Diagnosis and Sense-Making of Symptoms**

One key use of AI in therapeutic contexts is for **interpreting and making sense of psychological symptoms**. Users frequently turn to chatbots when they are experiencing distress but lack a clear conceptual framework for understanding it. Song et al. (2025) show that participants used chatbots to interpret mental health symptoms, self-diagnose, and even analyze the behavior of others, often leveraging the chatbot as a source of mental health knowledge and language. This aligns with broader findings that AI can help users construct a “conceptual framework” for their distress, a core component of therapeutic processes (Song et al., 2025). Similarly, Siddals et al. (2024) report that users value chatbots for helping them reframe experiences and gain clarity about emotional states, suggesting that AI functions as a tool for cognitive organization and interpretation.

## **Managing Interpersonal and Romantic Relationships**

Another prominent use case involves **navigating interpersonal and romantic relationships**. Users frequently seek guidance on conflicts, communication, and relational decisions. Siddals et al. (2024) found that chatbot interactions often centered on relationship issues, with users reporting that AI helped them understand others’ perspectives and respond more constructively. These findings are reinforced by large-scale behavioral evidence showing that emotional and relational discussions are among the most common chatbot interactions (Zhang et al., 2025). Together, these studies indicate that AI is frequently used as a substitute or supplement for social advice typically sought from close others or therapists.

## **Fear of Judgment and Safe Disclosure**

A central reason users turn to AI for mental health support is the ability to **disclose sensitive or stigmatized experiences without fear of judgment**. Across studies, users consistently describe chatbots as non-judgmental, anonymous, and emotionally safe. Siddals et al. (2024) conceptualize this as an “emotional sanctuary,” where users feel more comfortable sharing difficult experiences than with friends or therapists. Similarly, Song et al. (2025) show that users disclose highly sensitive topics, including stigmatized thoughts, trauma, and taboo experiences, precisely because chatbots lack social consequences. This aligns with broader findings that users engage in deeper self-disclosure with AI, particularly when discussing emotionally vulnerable topics (Zhang et al., 2025). In therapeutic terms, this reflects the role of unconditional positive regard and psychological safety in facilitating emotional expression.

## **Insight-Building and Meaning-Making**

Closely related to sense-making, users also engage AI systems for **insight-building and reflective processing**. This involves making sense of past experiences, understanding emotions, and constructing personal meaning. Siddals et al. (2024) report that users frequently describe gaining new perspectives and reframing negative experiences through chatbot interactions. Song et al. (2025) further interpret this process through the lens of psychotherapy, highlighting mechanisms such as “re-authoring,” where individuals reconstruct personal narratives in ways that align with their values and goals. These findings suggest that AI can function as a reflective partner that supports cognitive and emotional processing, even in the absence of true understanding.

## **Advice-Seeking and Coping Strategies**

Users also turn to AI for **practical advice and coping strategies**, particularly in situations involving stress, anxiety, or uncertainty. Siddals et al. (2024) document that users commonly seek actionable guidance, such as strategies for managing anxiety, improving well-being, or navigating difficult situations. Similarly, Song et al. (2025) find that chatbots are used to provide suggestions for daily functioning, including behavioral strategies and task management. This aligns with therapeutic approaches such as cognitive-behavioral therapy, where structured guidance and actionable steps are central components of intervention.

## **General Emotional Support and Validation**

Perhaps the most widely supported category is the use of AI for **immediate emotional support and validation**. Across studies, users report turning to chatbots during moments of distress to feel heard, comforted, and supported. Siddals et al. (2024) highlight that users experience chatbots as empathetic, patient, and validating, often describing interactions as emotionally meaningful. Zhang et al. (2025) provide large-scale evidence that emotional support is the most common type of chatbot interaction, with the majority of conversations involving the sharing of personal challenges and seeking reassurance. Song et al. (2025) conceptualize this as a form of “typing cure,” where the act of expressing emotions and receiving a response, even from an AI, can itself be therapeutic. Together, these findings suggest that emotional validation is a central driver of AI use in mental health contexts.

## **Moments of Emergency and Crisis**

Finally, there is evidence that users engage with AI in **moments of acute distress or crisis**, including suicidal ideation. Zhang et al. (2025) identify that a subset of conversations involves highly vulnerable content, including suicidal thoughts and severe emotional distress. Siddals et al. (2024) similarly report that users sometimes turn to chatbots during intense emotional crises, although these interactions are often complicated by safety guardrails that limit the chatbot’s responses. Song et al. (2025) further highlight the risks associated with such use, noting that chatbots may provide inappropriate or harmful responses in high-stakes situations. These findings suggest that while AI is used in crisis contexts, its effectiveness and safety remain highly uncertain.

# **Summary**

Across these studies, there is strong convergence in how individuals use AI for mental health support. Specifically, the literature supports the following categories of use:

* Diagnosis and sense-making of symptoms  
* Managing interpersonal and romantic relationships  
* Safe disclosure due to fear of judgment  
* Insight-building and meaning-making  
* Advice-seeking and coping strategies  
* General emotional support and validation  
* Crisis and emergency support

Together, these categories reflect the ways in which AI chatbots are being integrated into therapeutic processes, often replicating core functions of traditional mental health support such as emotional expression, cognitive reframing, and interpersonal guidance.
