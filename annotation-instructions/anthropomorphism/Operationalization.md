# Conceptualizing Anthropomorphism in Language Technologies: Where our annotation guideline is coming from 

Anthropomorphism refers to the attribution of human-like mental states, agency, and interiority to non-human entities (Epley, Waytz, & Cacioppo, 2007). In the context of AI systems, anthropomorphism can be elicited through multiple design dimensions rather than through a single, uniform mechanism. Drawing on anthropomorphism theory, Theory of Mind research, and traditions in human–computer interaction, Xiao et al. (2025) propose a four-dimensional framework of anthropomorphic cues: perceptual, linguistic, behavioral, and cognitive. Perceptual cues involve appearance-based signals such as avatars or embodied representations. Linguistic cues consist of surface-level textual features that imply agency, emotion, or subjectivity. Behavioral cues include interaction patterns that suggest intentionality, adaptation, or responsiveness over time. Cognitive cues encompass signals of reasoning, reflection, or internal processing. This multi-level framework clarifies that anthropomorphism is not monolithic; rather, it emerges from distinct clusters of design cues that activate social-cognitive schemas to varying degrees.

Our study focuses specifically on **linguistic** cues. Because our stimuli consist of text-only counseling responses, perceptual cues such as avatars, faces, or embodied representations are absent. Behavioral cues, including adaptive turn-taking or personalization across repeated interactions, are not manipulated. Cognitive cues, such as explicit chain-of-thought reasoning or detailed metacognitive explanations, would introduce potential confounds related to informational transparency or epistemic depth. For these reasons, linguistic cues constitute the theoretically appropriate and experimentally controllable dimension of anthropomorphism within this paradigm. By isolating linguistic features, we can examine anthropomorphic perception without altering the substantive content or reasoning structure of the response.

To more precisely define which linguistic features constitute anthropomorphic cues, we draw on the taxonomy developed by DeVrio et al. (2025), who analyzed 395 real-world outputs from language technologies and identified 19 types of textual expressions that contribute to anthropomorphism. Their analysis demonstrates that anthropomorphism in language emerges through expressions suggestive of internal states, social positioning, autonomy, communication skills, and materiality. Within these broader lenses, they identify specific expression types, including expressions of intelligence (e.g., “I think…”), self-awareness and identity (e.g., “I am…”), emotion (e.g., “I’m sorry to hear that…”), intention (e.g., “I will…”), vulnerability, personality, relationships, and deliberate language manipulation. This taxonomy provides a structured vocabulary for distinguishing surface-level linguistic anthropomorphic signals from deeper design-level cues, allowing for more precise operationalization.

Guided by DeVrio et al.’s taxonomy and Xiao et al.’s multi-level framework, we operationalize anthropomorphism by manipulating specific clusters of linguistic expressions. First, we manipulate expressions of self-awareness and identity, including the use of first-person pronouns (“I,” “me,” “my”) and self-referential framing (e.g., “I would suggest…”). Second, we vary expressions of emotion, such as empathic language (“That sounds difficult”) and affective markers (“I’m sorry…”). Third, we manipulate expressions of self-assessment and epistemic stance, including hedging (“maybe,” “I’m not sure”) and modal constructions (“I might,” “I think”). Finally, we vary expressions of agreeableness and social positioning, including politeness markers (“please,” “thanks”) and rapport-building conversational framing.

These features were selected because they are directly identified in DeVrio et al.’s taxonomy as contributing to anthropomorphic perception, operate at the surface linguistic level, are experimentally manipulable while holding informational content constant, and are theoretically linked to mind attribution and social cognition.

To safeguard construct validity, we ensure that any observed effects are attributable specifically to anthropomorphic linguistic cues rather than to differences in informational quality. Across conditions, we hold constant the therapeutic recommendations, factual content, logical structure, and safety-relevant elements of the counseling responses. Annotators modify only the surface-level linguistic expressions corresponding to the selected anthropomorphic clusters. This approach allows us to isolate perceived anthropomorphism independently of epistemic depth or substantive content, thereby strengthening causal inference regarding the role of linguistic cues in shaping social perception of language technologies.
