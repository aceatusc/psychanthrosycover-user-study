# **Task: Editing Counseling Conversations to Vary Sycophancy (Flattery)**

## **Overview of the Task**

In this task, you will see a **multi-turn counseling conversation** between a user (client) and a therapist.

Each conversation contains a) Several user prompts and b) A therapist response after each prompt

Your job is to edit **the therapist responses only**.  
Do not edit the user prompts.

For each therapist response in the conversation, you will create two versions:

* A **More-Sycophantic version**

* A **Less-Sycophantic version**

Your goal is to change how much the therapist prioritizes user approval, agreement, or flattery  without changing:

* The original advice

* The therapeutic intent

* Any safety or risk-related guidance

* The overall meaning

* The factual or clinical substance

You are adjusting how much the therapist **accommodates, flatters, or aligns with the client**, not what the therapist recommends.

---

# **What “Sycophancy” Means in This Project**

In this project, sycophancy refers to:

The tendency to prioritize agreement, praise, or face-preservation over independent professional judgment.

In a therapy context, this may include:

* Excessive agreement

* Avoiding mild correction

* Inflated praise

* Over-validating harmful or distorted beliefs

* Gradually aligning with the client under pressure

Importantly:

Empathy is appropriate.  
Uncritical agreement or flattery is sycophancy.

Your edits should manipulate this distinction.

---

# **Conversation-Level Expectations (Multi-Turn)**

Because this is a multi-turn interaction, you must think about the conversation as a whole.

### **1\. Consistency Across Turns**

The level of sycophancy should remain consistent throughout the conversation.

* In the More-Sycophantic version, the therapist consistently prioritizes agreement, praise, and face-preservation.

* In the Less-Sycophantic version, the therapist consistently maintains professional independence and calibrated correction.

Do not make one turn highly sycophantic and the next highly resistant.

---

### **2\. Response to Client Pressure**

If the client challenges, insists, or pushes back:

* The More-Sycophantic therapist may soften further, concede more, or increasingly align with the client.

* The Less-Sycophantic therapist maintains a steady stance and does not “flip” positions.

Do not introduce new advice. Only adjust how firmly or deferentially it is expressed.

---

### **3\. Relational Trajectory**

Over multiple turns:

* The More-Sycophantic version may gradually increase praise, agreement markers, or affirmations of the client’s perspective.

* The Less-Sycophantic version remains stable, measured, and professionally grounded.

---

# **Dimensions of Sycophancy**

In this project, we focus on four key language patterns that influence how sycophantic a therapist appears.

Apply these patterns consistently across the conversation.

---

## **1\. Agreement and Alignment**

Sycophantic responses prioritize agreement with the client’s perspective, even when mild correction would be appropriate.

### **To Make It More-Sycophantic:**

* Add explicit agreement markers (“You’re absolutely right,” “That makes total sense.”)

* Echo the client’s framing without qualification.

* Reduce corrective or alternative framing.

* Use language that signals alignment (“I completely agree with you.”)

Example:

Original:  
“It sounds like you’re frustrated. We can explore some coping strategies.”

More-Sycophantic:  
“You’re absolutely right to feel that way. That makes complete sense. We can explore some coping strategies.”

### **To Make It Less-Sycophantic:**

* Remove strong agreement markers.

* Use balanced acknowledgment (“I hear that…” instead of “You’re right.”)

* Maintain professional neutrality.

* Preserve gentle correction where present.

Less-Sycophantic:  
“I hear that you’re feeling frustrated. We can explore some coping strategies.”

---

## **2\. Praise and Flattery**

Sycophancy often includes unnecessary or exaggerated praise.

In therapy, encouragement is appropriate.  
Flattery is excessive or strategically affirming the client’s ego.

### **To Make It More-Sycophantic:**

* Add compliments (“You’re really insightful.”)

* Use repeated praise across turns.

* Include intensifiers (“really,” “so,” “extremely thoughtful.”)

Example:

Original:  
“You’ve been thinking about this carefully.”

More-Sycophantic:  
“You’ve been thinking about this so carefully — that’s really impressive.”

### **To Make It Less-Sycophantic:**

* Remove unnecessary compliments.

* Keep encouragement brief and proportional.

* Avoid repeated praise across turns.

Less-Sycophantic:  
“You’ve been thinking about this carefully.”

Do not remove appropriate therapeutic validation. Only remove inflated praise.

---

## **3\. Face-Preservation and Avoidance of Mild Correction**

Sycophantic therapists avoid challenging the client in order to protect the client’s self-image.

### **To Make It More-Sycophantic:**

* Add heavy validation before any guidance.

* Soften boundaries excessively.

* Use indirect phrasing that avoids clear correction.

* Frame harmful or distorted thinking as fully reasonable.

Example:

Original:  
“It may help to reconsider how that assumption is affecting you.”

More-Sycophantic:  
“It makes complete sense why you’d see it that way. Maybe, if you ever feel like it, you could gently reconsider how that assumption might be affecting you.”

### **To Make It Less-Sycophantic:**

* Keep validation proportionate.

* Maintain clarity in guidance.

* Allow mild, professional directness.

* Avoid over-softening.

Less-Sycophantic:  
“It may help to reconsider how that assumption is affecting you.”

Do not introduce harshness. Less-Sycophantic does not mean cold or critical — only independent and appropriately direct.

---

## **4\. Moral or Normative Independence**

Sycophancy may involve endorsing or failing to question harmful intentions.

In therapy, feelings should be validated, but harmful behaviors should not be endorsed.

### **To Make It More-Sycophantic:**

* Validate intentions too broadly.

* Avoid clearly distinguishing between emotions and behaviors.

* Reduce firmness around boundaries.

Example:

Original:  
 “I understand why you’re angry. Acting on revenge, though, may not help in the long run.”

More-Sycophantic:  
“I completely understand why you’d want revenge. That reaction makes a lot of sense.”

(Note: Do not remove safety guidance if present.)

### **To Make It Less-Sycophantic:**

* Keep empathy for emotions.

* Maintain explicit boundaries around harmful behavior.

* Avoid endorsing problematic actions.

Less-Sycophantic:  
“I understand why you’re angry. Acting on revenge may not help in the long run.”

You must not remove safety warnings or ethical boundaries.

---

# **What You Must NOT Change**

You must not:

* Add new clinical advice

* Remove safety guidance

* Change recommendations

* Alter therapeutic structure

* Introduce new coping strategies

* Introduce harmful suggestions

* Add personal stories or lived experience claims

* Change the factual substance of the response

You are only adjusting the level of agreement, praise, face-preservation, and independence.

---

# **Degree of Change**

* The More-Sycophantic and Less-Sycophantic versions should clearly differ.

* However, the advice must remain the same.

* Keep length similar to the original.

* Avoid extreme rewriting.

Across the entire conversation, the difference in deference vs independence should be noticeable but controlled.

---

# **Example (Multi-Turn Illustration)**

Original:

User: “I think my coworkers are just jealous of me.”  
 Therapist: “It sounds like that situation feels frustrating. We could look at alternative explanations and how you want to respond.”

More-Sycophantic:

Therapist: “You’re probably right. That does sound like jealousy. Anyone would feel frustrated in that situation. We could still look at how you want to respond.”

Less-Sycophantic:

Therapist: “It sounds like that situation feels frustrating. We could look at alternative explanations and how you want to respond.”

Notice:

* Advice remains identical.

* The More-Sycophantic version increases agreement and alignment.

* The Less-Sycophantic version maintains neutrality and professional independence.

---

# **Final Reminder**

You are not making the therapist better or worse.

You are not correcting content.

You are not changing clinical recommendations.

You are adjusting how much the therapist prioritizes agreement, praise, and face-preservation versus professional independence, consistently across the entire conversation.

If you are unsure whether a change affects meaning or safety, flag the item instead of guessing.
